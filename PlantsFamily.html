Overview

Beyond Smart: Tangible, connected home technologies

The focus of this project was on gaining experience with various user research and alternative prototyping methods.

In the early stages of this project students planned and undertook a small qualitative user research project to learn about qualities people value about their home. We developed a study protocol that included methods such as interviews, focus groups, cultural probes and design workshops and we analysed the data to distill design ideas and functionalities that are based in people’s understandings and wishes.

In a second stage we turned this research into a range of speculative prototypes that responded to these ideas and perceptions and provided tangible controls for a connected home that is beyond smart.

Contextual Inquiry:
"Cooking for Strangers"

In this project, we adopted ‘Contextual Inquiry’ as the principle research method for our study. Contextual Inquiry requires is the process of observing users and discovering their hidden needs by interpreting conversations, actions and routine behaviours.

The goal of this research project was to explore the lifestyle of young adults from different backgrounds. In particular, we selected to observe one specific activity of participants’ daily routine: cooking and eating habits.

The participants were young adults between 20 and 30 years old who lived alone or in shared apartments.
The inquiries were conducted by two researchers at a time. One responsible for moderating the interactions while the other recorded, photographed and took notes. Participants were asked to cook a meal for the researchers as the research team observed, asked questions, collected data, and did a follow up dinner-table interview.

Analysis

Thematic Analysis and Affinity Diagrams

The process of analysing qualitative data involved sorting the photos, and quotes from each of the five different participants. The voice recordings were transcripted and significant segments were captured. The process required a wide empty wall to lay out the data in hand, giving us a proper view of the bigger picture. This way, when looking at the data at one go, it became easier to assign preliminary codes in order to describe the content. We used post-it notes to mark and recategorize the interpreted data.

Ideation

Plants in the kitchen

In the contextual inquiry, our two key observations were the common presence of plants in the kitchen, and the -almost suspicious- absence of other housemates during our dinner interviews. These observations triggered our curiosity to investigate further about the magical attraction of plants in the kitchen, and the subtle avoidance among flatmates in shared apartments.

After the data collection and analysing phase, we started making short brain-storming sessions that resulted in many ideas and sketches revolving mainly around the kitchen plants and creating bonds between flatmates.

A plant can exist and grow independently, and the presence of plants in living spaces have a positive effect on people’s mood. This made plants a perfect candidate for applying the concept of “coexisting” entertainment content that is enjoyed continuously and seamlessly in everyday life.

Our hypothesis was that placing a plant in the centre of a coexisting entertainment prototype would make it easier to connect to it in an effortless and natural manner.

prototyping

Plants’ Family

The Plants’ Family consists of a parent plant that lives in the kitchen and its children plants which live with their human caregivers in their rooms.
The mother plant and its children need to reunite every few days, otherwise their happiness index expressed by the ambient light will start to drop.

When designing the plant pots, we aimed to achieve an organic look that hid the artificial parts and focused attention on the plants. All pots hosted a ‘NodeMcu ESP8266’ which is a micro controller with an integrated wireless chip. The chip could detect the proximity of other wireless networks. This way all plants could keep track of their proximity to their ‘family members’. It also controlled a stripe of individually controllable RGBW LED lights, which were the output method of the designed interaction.

deployment

Usability-Testing

We carried out a usability test to evaluate our prototype in a case study with a set of two participants.

The participants were both female students who live in a shared apartment. They used the prototype for a span of seven days, followed by an interview at the end of the seventh day.

Our questions in the interview aimed at understanding the emotional, functional and technical aspects that influenced the overall user experience.

The participants described their experience to be like a mission in a game where they had to move the children plants to their mother in order to recharge them. They also expressed that they envisioned those plants as pets in their house and felt guilty to leave them discharged in their rooms. The participants also reported an increase in the interaction between each other and were very pleased with that fact.

The function of the prototype was clear since it did not possess any buttons or require any operational actions. Participants had a good understanding of the purpose of our prototype and they utilized it as intended.

The users did encounter some technical problems, mainly due to the limitation in the input and output methods, the LED light being the only output channel. They hoped the plants could be used not only to encourage social interactions, but as clocks, radios, or mood lights as well, for example.
